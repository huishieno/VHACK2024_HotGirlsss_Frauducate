How to Report
Scammers

Reporting a scam is a vital step in protecting yourself and others. By sharing your experience, you can help authorities track down scammers and prevent future attacks.
This page provides guidance for reporting scams depending on the type and where it occurred.

  1. Document Everything:
    As soon as you realize you've been scammed, gather all relevant documentation. This includes emails, messages, receipts, bank statements, wire transfer records, or any other evidence related to the scam.
  2. Contact Your Financial Institution:
    If the scam involved unauthorized transactions on your bank account or credit card, immediately contact your bank or credit card issuer. They can help you freeze your account, dispute fraudulent charges, and prevent further unauthorized activity.
  3. File a Report with Law Enforcement:
    Contact your local police department or the appropriate law enforcement agency. Provide them with all the evidence you've gathered. They may file a report and initiate an investigation, although the outcome can vary depending on the jurisdiction and the nature of the scam.
  4. Report to Federal Agencies:
    Depending on the nature of the scam, you may need to report it to federal agencies such as:
    • Federal Trade Commission (FTC):
      File a complaint with the FTC through their website or by calling their toll-free number. The FTC collects complaints about various types of fraud and shares them with law enforcement agencies.
    • Internet Crime Complaint Center (IC3):
      If the scam involved online activity, such as phishing or online fraud, report it to IC3, a partnership between the FBI and the National White Collar Crime Center. You can file a complaint on their website.
    • Financial Crimes Enforcement Network (FinCEN):
      If the scam involved money laundering or suspicious financial activities, you can submit a report to FinCEN online.
  5. Report to Consumer Protection Agencies:
    In addition to federal agencies, you can also report financial scams to consumer protection agencies in your country or region. They may provide assistance and advocate on your behalf. Research and find the appropriate agency for your location.
  6. Report to Online Platforms:
    If the scam occurred on an online platform such as social media, online marketplace, or auction site, report the scammer to the platform's customer support or abuse team. They may take action against the scammer and prevent them from victimizing others.
  7. Notify Credit Bureaus (if necessary):
    If the scam involved identity theft or compromised personal information, consider placing a fraud alert or a credit freeze on your credit report. This can help prevent further unauthorized accounts or transactions from being opened in your name.
  8. Seek Legal Advice (if necessary):
    Depending on the severity and complexity of the scam, you may want to consult with a legal professional who specializes in financial fraud. They can provide guidance on your rights, potential legal actions, and steps to recover any losses.
  9. Educate Othersn:
    Share your experience with friends, family, and online communities to raise awareness about the scam and help others avoid falling victim to similar schemes.

    10. Contact Frauducate if you need further assistance.
